# Construct Replacement: AI Is Changing What Behavioral Data Measures

**Anthony Guzzardo**
April 2026

---

## The Problem Has a Name on the Wrong Side

When AI systems train on their own synthetic output, the training distribution degrades. This is model collapse, and it has a formal characterization, empirical demonstrations, and an active research program (1). The AI community recognized the threat quickly because it degrades AI capabilities, which is to say, it threatens the interests of the people building AI systems.

The same structural problem exists on the other side of the human-AI interface, and it has received almost no attention.

The behavioral and cognitive sciences depend on observing what humans do: what people write, how they write it, what they say, how they move, what they choose. The validity of every behavioral measurement rests on a foundational assumption: that the observed behavior reflects the cognitive process the instrument claims to measure. AI mediation of human input is breaking this assumption across every behavioral domain where AI assists, suggests, completes, or co-produces human output. The failure is not that the data is getting noisier. The failure is that the data now measures something else.

## Noise versus Replacement

The distinction is categorical, and it matters because the two problems require completely different responses.

**Noise** is measurement imprecision. The construct being measured is intact; the instrument captures it with error. A bathroom scale that reads two pounds heavy on every measurement is noisy. The construct (body weight) is unchanged. You can average out random noise and correct systematic bias.

**Construct replacement** means the measurement now indexes a different construct entirely. The surface data looks identical to what the instrument was designed to capture. The generating process is different. More data does not help, because you are accumulating evidence about the wrong thing with increasing statistical power. A bathroom scale on a trampoline during an earthquake is not noisily measuring body weight. It is cleanly measuring something else. The readings are precise. They are precisely wrong.

AI mediation of human input produces construct replacement, not noise. When a person retrieves a word from memory and types it, the observable data (the word, the timing of each keystroke, the pauses) reflects lexical retrieval, orthographic encoding, and motor execution. When the same word appears because the person accepted a predictive text suggestion, the surface output is identical: same word, same position, same sentence. But the generating process is a perceptual-evaluative task (scan suggestion, compare to intention, accept) rather than a retrieval-production task. It engages different cognitive resources, operates on a different timescale, and generates a different temporal signature.

This is not a theoretical concern. Arnold, Chauncey, and Gajos demonstrated that participants writing with predictive text produced shorter texts with fewer unpredicted words, fewer adjectives, and reduced lexical variety, not because they had smaller vocabularies, but because the suggestion interface shifted the task from generation to selection (2). The participants' language did not become noisier. It became a different kind of language, generated by a different process, measuring a different thing.

The replacement scales beyond individual words. Doshi and Hauser found that AI-assisted creative writing enhances individual output quality while significantly reducing collective diversity: different writers producing AI-assisted text converge toward similar outputs (3). The individual measurement looks better. The population distribution is compressed. Padmakumar and He showed this homogenization operates asymmetrically, with AI suggestions causing non-Western writers to adopt Western writing conventions (4).

Most critically, the replacement persists after the AI is removed. Zhou and Liu conducted a seven-day experiment in which participants generated ideas with and without AI assistance. After AI withdrawal, individual creativity dropped, but content homogeneity continued climbing (5). The AI had not temporarily altered the output. It had restructured the generating process in the human. A behavioral dataset collected from a population with extensive AI-mediation experience may not reflect unmediated cognition even when the immediate data collection is unassisted.

This is the feature that separates construct replacement from any form of noise. Noise disappears when the noise source is removed. Construct replacement can be permanent.

## The Contamination Is Invisible

No major behavioral dataset records whether a given word was typed or accepted from a suggestion. No keystroke logging system flags which pauses reflect retrieval difficulty and which reflect suggestion scanning. No linguistic corpus marks which sentences were composed by the writer and which were AI-drafted and lightly edited. No clinical self-report instrument records whether the respondent used AI assistance.

This is not a gap that better analysis can close. AI-assisted text is harder to detect than fully AI-generated text by construction: it genuinely is human-produced, by a human who intended to write it, under cognitive conditions that differ from what the instrument assumes. The distinguishing information exists only in process data (keystroke timing, pause structure, acceptance events), and that data is not collected by any standard behavioral dataset.

The contamination is baked in at collection. It cannot be removed because it was never recorded. And no existing data provenance framework requires recording it.

## The Window

The practical consequence is that naturalistic unassisted behavioral data is becoming unavailable on a timeline that outpaces the fields that need it.

The digital biomarker field has been building toward passive, longitudinal, ambient behavioral monitoring: keystroke dynamics during natural writing, speech patterns during ordinary conversation, gait during daily life. All of these assume that the observed behavior is the person's own. If what the person does anyway is AI-mediated, the ecological validity premise collapses. Laboratory conditions can require unassisted performance, but laboratory conditions are not what ecological validity means.

The urgency is sharpened by the Zhou and Liu finding. If AI mediation leaves a lasting imprint on human generative processes, then the window is not just about capturing unmediated behavior while it still occurs. It is about establishing baselines in populations whose cognitive processes have not yet been restructured by sustained AI exposure. That window is narrower still.

## What Is Needed

This argument does not claim that AI mediation is harmful. AI-assisted writing can be more efficient, more accessible, and more effective. The argument is strictly about measurement validity: whether the data produced under AI-mediated conditions is interpretable under the assumptions the field has been making. It may not be.

Three things are needed now.

**Empirical characterization.** For each form of AI mediation (word-level autocomplete, phrase-level suggestion, sentence-level drafting, full document co-production), determine where it falls on the noise-to-replacement continuum using paired within-subject designs that compare process-level behavioral signals under mediated and unmediated conditions. This work does not yet exist.

**Provenance infrastructure.** AI-mediation status must be recorded as a variable in behavioral datasets, alongside demographics and task conditions. No new technology is required. Only the recognition that mediation status is a relevant variable.

**Baseline accumulation.** Instruments that capture unmediated human behavior at process-level fidelity, under longitudinal architectures, must be built now, while unmediated behavior is still common enough to serve as ground truth. Pre-2020 behavioral datasets collected before widespread AI mediation should be recognized as a limited, irreplaceable resource and preserved with the same urgency the field has brought to pre-synthetic training data on the output side.

The behavioral sciences have a data problem they have not named. It is not the problem of insufficient data, biased samples, or unreliable instruments. It is the problem that the construct being measured is changing underneath the measurement, invisibly, at scale, with no provenance record. Naming it is the first step toward addressing it.
