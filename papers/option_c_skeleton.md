# Option C: Skeleton and Working Document

**Working title (placeholder):** *The Signal and the Silence: What Happens to Cognitive Biomarkers When Humans Stop Producing Their Own Language*

**Status:** Not yet drafted. This document describes what the paper aims to be, what it needs to argue, what literatures must be engaged, and what work must be done before a first draft is possible.

---

## 1. What This Paper Is

A position paper — likely essayistic in register rather than formal-academic — that makes the following central move:

The digital cognitive biomarker field has been organized around a question that is becoming structurally obsolete. The question is: *how can we passively detect cognitive decline by observing the behavioral outputs of human cognition?* The assumption underneath that question is that human cognition produces a relatively stable stream of behavioral outputs — writing, speech, movement, decisions — that can be monitored over time. AI-mediation of those outputs does not merely add noise to the signal. It changes what is being measured. When language is co-produced with a model, behavioral biomarkers derived from language are no longer measuring the human's cognitive state in the way the field has assumed. They are measuring a hybrid system in which the human's role is increasingly that of editor, acceptor, or director rather than producer.

The paper's claim is not that this invalidates cognitive biomarkers. It is that the field has a choice it has not yet recognized: to keep measuring what it has been measuring and accept that the construct is shifting underneath the instrument, or to reframe what the instrument is for. The paper proposes that the second path leads somewhere more interesting: instruments that track the *degree* to which a person is still producing their own language, thought, and decisions — not as a proxy for neurological health alone, but as a measurable dimension of human cognitive engagement in an AI-mediated world.

The keystroke-cognition literature, the demographic confound, and the Alice project all fit inside this frame as specific cases. The paper should treat them as such rather than as the frame itself.

## 2. The Core Argument, in Skeleton

**Premise 1.** Passive behavioral biomarkers assume that the behavior being observed reflects the cognitive state of the person being observed.

**Premise 2.** AI-mediation of behavior — autocomplete, predictive text, AI-assisted drafting, voice assistants that complete tasks, decision-support systems — is rapidly weakening the coupling between observed behavior and the cognitive state of the individual. The behavior is increasingly co-produced.

**Premise 3.** This is not a noise problem to be engineered around. It is a construct validity problem. What is being measured is changing.

**Premise 4.** The usual response to construct validity problems is to either refine the construct or refine the instrument. The field has not yet done either, because the problem has not been named at this level of generality.

**Claim 1.** There is an emerging and distinct clinical and public-health question that has not yet been articulated: *to what degree is this person still the cognitive source of their own behavior?* This question is separable from neurodegeneration, separable from mental illness, and separable from ordinary tool-use. It is the question of unmediated cognitive engagement.

**Claim 2.** This question is plausibly measurable — imperfectly, at the edges, and only in people who still produce some unmediated behavior. Keystroke dynamics during unassisted writing is one way. There are others, probably including patterns of decision-making, variation in language style across contexts, the structure of revision, and behavioral responses to prompts designed to elicit unmediated production.

**Claim 3.** Instruments designed around this reframed question have different properties than instruments designed around neurodegeneration detection: they are longitudinal by necessity, they benefit from rather than resist ecological validity, they assume heterogeneous populations rather than clinical cohorts, and they make the AI-mediation of behavior a measured variable rather than a confound to be controlled.

**Claim 4 (tentative, and the one most likely to get pushback).** The decline of unmediated cognitive engagement may itself be a measurable phenomenon worth tracking at population scale. Whether it is pathological, adaptive, or neutral is an open empirical and ethical question. But the framework for measuring it should exist before a judgment about it is possible.

## 3. Audience and Register

**Who this paper is for.** Not the keystroke-cognition specialist audience of Option A. The audience here is broader — digital biomarker researchers, cognitive scientists interested in distributed cognition, philosophers of mind working on AI, journalists and essayists tracking the cultural dimensions of AI adoption, and technically-literate readers who have noticed the same phenomenon in their own lives and don't yet have vocabulary for it.

**Register.** Essayistic rather than formal-academic. The paper should read more like a long-form essay in *The Atlantic*, *The New Atlantis*, *Aeon*, or *The New Yorker* than a conventional research paper — while still holding itself to a standard of citation and argumentation. This is a harder register to hit than it looks and is the single biggest stylistic risk for the paper. Models worth studying: L.M. Sacasas's newsletter essays on technology and human formation, Paul Kingsnorth's cultural criticism, Ted Chiang's essays on AI (especially "ChatGPT Is a Blurry JPEG of the Web"), and the better essays in *The Point* and *Harper's* on technology and thought. The goal is essayistic readability that still earns intellectual seriousness; the failure mode is either dry academicese or untethered cultural complaint.

**Length target.** Probably 8,000 to 12,000 words. Long enough to develop the argument, short enough to be read in one sitting. A version at 5,000 words for a more general publication is plausible later but is not the right first target.

## 4. Literatures That Must Be Engaged

This is the part that requires work before drafting. The Option A paper sat inside a research literature the author had already read. Option C sits at the intersection of several literatures that need serious engagement before the paper can claim to be doing more than pattern-matching.

**Extended mind and distributed cognition.** Clark and Chalmers (1998), "The Extended Mind." Andy Clark, *Supersizing the Mind* (2008) and *Surfing Uncertainty* (2016). Edwin Hutchins, *Cognition in the Wild* (1995). Evan Thompson on embodied cognition. The core challenge from this literature: cognition has always been distributed across brain, body, tools, and environment. The paper must argue either that AI-mediation is categorically different from prior offloading, or that it is continuous but occurring at a rate and scale that produces qualitatively different effects. The second argument is probably easier to defend but requires engagement with the tradition rather than dismissal.

**Philosophy of technology and human formation.** L.M. Sacasas (*The Convivial Society* newsletter), Shannon Vallor (*Technology and the Virtues*, 2016), Albert Borgmann (*Technology and the Character of Contemporary Life*, 1984), Ivan Illich (*Tools for Conviviality*, 1973). These authors have been arguing for decades that tools shape the people who use them in ways that go beyond instrumental effect. The paper should engage directly with the strongest version of this argument, not a strawman of it.

**History of technology-and-cognition moral panics.** Socrates's argument against writing in the *Phaedrus*. Elizabeth Eisenstein on the printing revolution. Neil Postman's *Amusing Ourselves to Death* (1985) and *Technopoly* (1992). Nicholas Carr, *The Shallows* (2010). The paper must take seriously the objection that each generation has worried about the new technology in similar terms and that the worries have only been partly vindicated. The strongest anti-panic argument is that humans adapted, new cognitive forms emerged, and the worries turned out to be about transitions rather than endings. The paper needs to say why AI is or is not different, without merely asserting that it is.

**Empirical work on AI-assisted writing and cognition.** Beyond Banovic, Quinn and Zhai, and Buschek (already cited in Option A), this includes more recent work on LLM effects on writing, ideation, and learning outcomes. The literature here is young and growing fast; a targeted search in the months immediately preceding drafting is necessary. Candidate sources include research out of Microsoft Research on Copilot effects, studies on GitHub Copilot and code comprehension, and the emerging "human-AI collaboration" literature.

**Digital biomarkers beyond keystroke dynamics.** Actigraphy, mobility data (e.g., Kourtis et al. on smartphone-based cognitive biomarkers), speech-based longitudinal work (Winterlight Labs, DementiaBank), and the broader "passive sensing" literature. The paper needs to generalize its argument beyond keystroke dynamics, so it must show how the AI-mediation problem applies to each of these modalities — voice assistants mediating speech, navigation apps mediating spatial decision-making, recommendation systems mediating preference expression.

**Public health frameworks for behavioral phenomena that are not diseases.** How does the field talk about measurable behavioral patterns that may not rise to the level of diagnosable conditions — loneliness, sedentary behavior, sleep deprivation, screen time? The paper's Claim 4 (that declining unmediated cognitive engagement may be worth tracking at population scale) needs to situate itself inside an existing public-health framework for "measurable but not clinical" phenomena, or explain why it needs a new one.

**Cognitive reserve and use-it-or-lose-it.** The literature on cognitive reserve (Stern and colleagues) and the empirical question of whether cognitive engagement in midlife affects cognitive outcomes in later life. This is directly relevant to Claim 4: if there is evidence that sustained cognitive effort maintains function and reduced effort accelerates decline, the reframing from "detecting decline" to "measuring engagement" has a clearer clinical stake. If the evidence is weaker than the popular framing suggests, the paper needs to be careful not to overclaim.

## 5. The Hard Questions the Paper Must Answer

These are the questions a serious reader will ask. The paper cannot avoid them, and most are not yet answered in the author's current thinking.

**Q1. What distinguishes AI-mediation from prior forms of cognitive offloading?** Writing externalized memory. Calculators externalized arithmetic. Search engines externalized recall. Each was argued at the time to be corrosive to human cognition and was, in hindsight, partially so and partially enabling. Why is AI different? Candidate answers: (a) the completeness of the offloading — AI can do the full cognitive task, not a sub-component; (b) the invisibility of the offloading — users may not notice they have delegated cognition; (c) the scope — AI can be applied to almost any cognitive task, not a bounded domain; (d) the speed of adoption, which outpaces the adaptation mechanisms that made prior technology shifts survivable. The paper should commit to one or two of these, not all four, and argue for them.

**Q2. Is "unmediated cognition" even a coherent concept?** The extended mind tradition says no — cognition is always mediated by something. The paper needs a position here. One option: concede the point, and redefine the concept as "the degree to which a person's cognition is mediated by artifacts that do versus do not themselves perform cognition." Pen and paper do not perform cognition; LLMs do. This is a distinction the extended mind literature mostly does not make explicitly.

**Q3. How do you measure "degree of mediation" without being intrusive or paternalistic?** The paper proposes that this is measurable in principle, but gets vague about how. Serious readers will want at least a sketch of what the measurement looks like. This is where keystroke dynamics, revision patterns, and calibration deltas come back in as examples — not as the whole answer, but as existence proofs.

**Q4. What is the clinical or public-health actionability?** If the paper is arguing that declining unmediated cognitive engagement is a phenomenon worth tracking, the obvious question is: so what? What would anyone do about it? The answer cannot just be "more research." The paper probably needs to gesture at: individual-level feedback for people who want to track their own engagement, clinical relevance for understanding cognitive trajectories in aging populations, and population-level monitoring that could inform policy around AI deployment in education and workplaces. Each of these has its own set of ethical issues that must be named.

**Q5. How does the paper avoid being read as anti-AI moral panic?** This is the biggest stylistic risk. The argument must make room for AI-mediation as adaptive, valuable, and sometimes preferable to unmediated cognition. Writing a letter with AI help is not worse than writing it alone; it is different, and sometimes better. The paper's argument is about measurement, not judgment. The register has to hold that distinction tightly. If the paper reads as nostalgic for a pre-AI cognitive purity, it has failed.

**Q6. Where does the author's instrument fit without dominating the paper?** Alice is one attempt at building toward measurement of the phenomenon the paper describes. It should be mentioned briefly, probably near the end, in an author's note or a clearly marked section. It should not be the climax of the paper. If the paper works, readers will ask what it would look like to build such an instrument; the author can answer that question in follow-up communications.

## 6. Tentative Structure (not final)

**Opening.** A concrete scene or case that grounds the abstract argument. Candidates: a description of a writer using an LLM to draft an email and what that process looks like from the inside, a reference to the Vishnevsky clock-drawing study as a way to show that cognitive assessments can expire, or a personal observation about the author's own writing habits across the last five years. The opening must be readable by someone who has never heard of keystroke dynamics.

**Part 1: The Question the Field Has Been Asking.** Brief recap of digital cognitive biomarkers, the promise of passive assessment, the keystroke-cognition literature. Enough to establish what the assumption is (behavior reflects cognition) without dwelling on methodological detail. This is the Option A material, radically compressed.

**Part 2: The Question the Field Isn't Asking.** The shift from "how do we detect decline" to "what are we measuring when behavior is increasingly co-produced with AI." The Banovic / Quinn / Buschek findings, but framed as indicators of a general phenomenon rather than a specific methodological challenge.

**Part 3: The Extended Mind and Its Limits.** Engagement with the philosophical tradition. Concession that cognition is always distributed, followed by the argument that AI-mediation is different in kind or degree. This is the intellectually hardest part of the paper.

**Part 4: A Reframing.** The proposal: instruments that measure the degree of unmediated cognitive engagement, longitudinally, at the individual level, as a distinct construct from neurodegeneration. Includes the keystroke/writing-process example as an existence proof, and sketches what the concept would look like applied to other modalities.

**Part 5: What Would Be Measured, and What Would It Mean.** Concrete discussion of what kinds of signals the proposed instruments would track, what the base-rate and construct-validity challenges are, and what actionability looks like at individual and population levels.

**Part 6: The Ethical Weight.** Measuring "unmediated cognitive engagement" is not neutral. It implies a view about what human cognition should be, and it risks pathologizing the normal use of valuable tools. The paper must name this directly and propose a framework for avoiding the worst versions of the risk.

**Closing.** Return to the opening scene, reframed. The paper should end with a question rather than a declaration — something like "what would it mean to take seriously, as a measurable phenomenon, the degree to which you are still the author of your own thoughts?"

## 7. What Needs to Happen Before Drafting

In rough order:

1. **Read Clark and Chalmers (1998) if not already read. Read at least the first two chapters of Clark's *Supersizing the Mind*.** The paper cannot engage the extended mind tradition secondhand.
2. **Read Sacasas's essays on attention, formation, and technology.** Specifically the ones on "the attentional commons" and on technology-and-character. His register is close to the register this paper should aim for.
3. **Read or reread Ted Chiang's "ChatGPT Is a Blurry JPEG of the Web."** Not because the argument is what this paper needs to make, but because it's a model of how to write seriously about AI for a general audience without collapsing into either hype or panic.
4. **Do a targeted search on 2024-2026 empirical work on LLM effects on writing, ideation, and cognitive task performance.** The field is moving fast and the paper cannot be written from 2023 literature.
5. **Draft a 500-word version of the central argument as a test.** If the argument cannot be made in 500 words, it is not yet clear enough to sustain a 10,000-word essay. If it can, those 500 words become the seed the full essay grows from.
6. **Decide on the opening scene.** The paper lives or dies on whether the first 500 words earn the reader's attention. This is not a decision to leave for the drafting stage.
7. **Decide how explicitly to position Alice in the paper.** Options range from "not mentioned at all, project named in a footnote" to "mentioned in passing in Part 5 as an example of the kind of instrument the argument implies." The author should decide before drafting rather than during.
8. **Decide on the publication venue, because it shapes everything.** *The New Atlantis*, *Aeon*, *Asterisk*, a substack post, an arXiv preprint, a formal peer-reviewed journal in philosophy of mind — each implies a different length, register, and citation style. This is the decision that should be made first and is currently unmade.

## 8. Risks and Failure Modes

**The paper becomes moral panic.** Biggest risk. Mitigation: hold the measurement-vs-judgment distinction tightly, include genuine cases where AI-mediation is good, avoid any language that treats unmediated cognition as morally superior.

**The paper is dismissed as dilettantism.** Second biggest risk. The paper wades into philosophy of mind, history of technology, cognitive science, and public health without full credentials in any. Mitigation: engage seriously with the relevant literatures, cite honestly, concede where the author is reasoning from outside the expert community, and write in a register that makes clear the paper is an argument offered for consideration rather than a pronouncement.

**The paper overclaims.** The empirical basis for Claim 4 in particular is thin. Mitigation: frame Claim 4 as a research program rather than an established finding, explicitly name what evidence would support or refute it.

**The paper is too long to be read and too short to be definitive.** Mitigation: optimize for readability within the 8,000-to-12,000-word range; accept that the paper's job is to name a phenomenon and propose a reframing, not to settle the question.

**The paper becomes a stalking horse for Alice.** Mitigation: write it as if Alice did not exist. If the argument only works when Alice is the implied answer, the argument is not strong enough.

## 9. Open Questions for the Author

These are questions only the author can answer. They should be resolved before drafting, not during.

- Is the claim that AI-mediation is categorically different from prior technologies, or just quantitatively different in ways that matter? The paper needs one answer, not both.
- How central should the keystroke-cognition case be? Is it the opening example, a running thread, or a single section among several?
- Is the paper making a predictive claim (this will matter in the next 10 years), a diagnostic claim (this is already happening and under-recognized), or a conceptual claim (the frame we are using is wrong)? It can do more than one, but there needs to be a primary claim.
- How much of the author's personal experience belongs in the paper? Essayistic register invites first-person observation; academic register forbids it. The paper is in between and needs to pick a point.
- Who, specifically, does the author want to read this and do something with it? Name three or four specific kinds of reader. The paper's shape will fall out of that list.
