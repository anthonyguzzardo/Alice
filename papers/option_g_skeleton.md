# Option G: Skeleton and Working Document

**Working title:** *Irreversible Loss: An Information-Theoretic Argument for Process-Level Cognitive Preservation*

**Status:** Skeleton. Not yet drafted.

---

## 1. What This Paper Is

An essay arguing that the loss of process-level cognitive data is information-theoretically irreversible, that this irreversibility has consequences for science, self-knowledge, and species-level understanding of biological intelligence, and that the reconstruction of writing behavior from process-level measurements (the Avatar) constitutes an existence proof that the data is worth preserving.

The central move: entropy destroys process data. Artifacts survive because they are compressed, static, and copyable. Process records, the temporal microstructure of a mind producing output in real time, are high-entropy, ephemeral, and never recorded by default. When the person stops producing them, or dies, the data is gone. Not degraded. Gone. The information cannot be reconstructed from the artifacts, because the artifacts are a lossy compression of the process. The compression is one-way. This is not a metaphor. It is an information-theoretic fact.

The essay uses historical figures (Franklin, Darwin, Dickinson, Ramanujan) not as nostalgia but as concrete demonstrations of what process data would have revealed that artifacts cannot. It then connects to the present: AI mediation is making unmediated process data uncollectable from living people, accelerating the loss on a timeline that outpaces recognition of its value.

The Avatar is the constructive argument: if process-level measurements are sufficient to reconstruct writing behavior, then process-level data is demonstrably information-rich. The reconstruction fidelity is the lower bound on the information content of the record. What the Avatar can reconstruct is what the data contains. What it cannot is what entropy has already taken or what the instrument has yet to capture.

**Relationship to the arc:**
- E names the process record and argues it is the most fundamental data
- G quantifies the loss and argues it is irreversible
- F proves the data is sufficient for reconstruction (the preservation works)
- C establishes the stakes (cognitive reserve, closing window)
- B names the threat (construct replacement makes process data uncollectable)

G is the urgency paper. E says what the data is. G says what happens when it is gone.

## 2. The Core Argument

**Premise 1.** There are three levels of record a biological intelligence produces: artifacts (what it made), genome (how to build the hardware), and process records (how the hardware actually ran). The artifact is a lossy compression of the process. The process contains the artifact but the artifact does not contain the process. This asymmetry is information-theoretic: the compression discards temporal microstructure, revision history, hesitation patterns, retrieval dynamics, and attentional allocation. None of these can be recovered from the finished product.

**Premise 2.** Entropy operates differently on each level. Artifacts are low-entropy, static objects that survive copying and storage. Genomes are encoded in a self-replicating substrate with error-correction mechanisms. Process records are high-entropy, temporally extended, and exist only in the moment of production. They are not recorded by default. They are not self-replicating. They are not copyable after the fact. Without active capture, they are lost at the rate of lived experience.

**Premise 3.** For every historical figure whose artifacts survive, the process record is permanently lost. We have what Franklin wrote. We do not have how he wrote it: the pauses before each word, the deletions, the reorderings, the moments of uncertainty, the retrieval dynamics that would characterize his cognitive architecture. This is not a matter of incomplete archives. It is a category of data that was never captured, because the capture technology did not exist.

**Claim 1: The loss is irreversible.** You cannot reconstruct the process from the artifact. This is not an engineering limitation. It is a consequence of the lossy compression between process and product. Two different cognitive processes can produce identical artifacts: a sentence retrieved fluently from a well-rehearsed argument and the same sentence arrived at through twenty minutes of hesitation, deletion, and reformulation produce identical text. The text survives. The process distinction is erased. No amount of future technology can recover what was never encoded in the surviving data.

**Claim 2: The data is demonstrably information-rich.** The Avatar reconstruction proves this. Process-level measurements (motor timing, pause architecture, revision topology, vocabulary transitions) contain enough structured information to reconstruct writing behavior. The reconstruction is imperfect but meaningful. The fidelity is the lower bound on the information content of the data. If you can rebuild the behavior from the measurements, the measurements contain something real about the person. This is not a theoretical claim. It is an empirical demonstration running in a Rust engine on a personal corpus.

**Claim 3: The capture window is closing.** AI mediation of writing is replacing the process that generates process records. When a person accepts a predictive text suggestion, the process record at that moment encodes suggestion evaluation, not lexical retrieval. When an LLM drafts a paragraph, there is no human process record for that paragraph at all. The fraction of human behavioral output that is unmediated is declining. With it declines the possibility of capturing process records from the population. This is not a future risk. It is a present trajectory.

**Claim 4: The value of existing unmediated data appreciates.** As unmediated process data becomes scarcer, its information-theoretic value increases. A corpus of unmediated writing process data from 2024-2026 will be, by 2035, a record of cognitive behavior that cannot be recollected from a population whose writing is predominantly AI-mediated. This is the deflationary supply argument: the supply of unmediated process data is decreasing while the demand for understanding unmediated cognition (clinical, scientific, historical) is increasing. The intersection defines a value curve that is monotonically increasing from this point forward.

**Claim 5: Preservation requires active instrumentation, not passive archiving.** Process records do not accumulate in libraries. They are not captured by existing data infrastructure. They require purpose-built instruments that record the temporal microstructure of behavior at millisecond resolution during natural practice. The instruments must satisfy the design constraints from Option D (unassisted input, process-level capture, n=1 baselines, practice autonomy). The instruments must exist before the window closes.

## 3. The Historical Demonstration

The essay uses specific historical figures not as nostalgia but as concrete information-theoretic demonstrations. Each example illustrates a different dimension of what process data would have revealed.

### Benjamin Franklin

We have the Autobiography, the letters, the scientific papers, Poor Richard's Almanack. What we do not have: the temporal dynamics of Franklin's composition. Franklin was a printer who became a writer who became a diplomat who became a scientist. His cognitive architecture shifted across domains and decades. The process record would show whether his writing process changed as he moved between domains, whether his revision patterns differed when writing persuasion versus science, whether his motor timing showed the kind of domain-transfer signatures that modern cognitive science studies in lab settings. The artifacts show what he produced in each domain. The process would show how his mind reorganized itself to produce it.

**Information-theoretic point:** Franklin's artifacts are well-preserved. The information loss is not in what survives but in what was never encoded. The process-to-artifact compression ratio for a figure with extensive surviving works is high: many terabytes of process data compressed into megabytes of text. The discarded information is not noise. It is the cognitive architecture.

### Emily Dickinson

1,800 poems, many with variant word choices noted in the manuscripts. The fascicles show she deliberated over specific words. But we do not know the temporal structure of that deliberation: how long she paused before each variant, whether she wrote the alternatives sequentially or returned to revise, whether the revision pattern changed across her productive decades. The manuscripts preserve the product of revision. They do not preserve the process. The word-choice variants are the only surviving trace of a process that, in a keystroke-logged environment, would have been fully captured.

**Information-theoretic point:** Dickinson's manuscripts accidentally preserved a fragment of process data (variant word choices) that most manuscripts do not. This fragment is considered among the most valuable aspects of her archive by literary scholars. It demonstrates that even partial process data is recognized as scientifically important. Full process data would be orders of magnitude more informative.

### Srinivasa Ramanujan

Notebooks full of results without proofs. The mathematical community has spent a century reconstructing how Ramanujan arrived at his formulas. The process is the mystery. The artifacts (the formulas) survive but the cognitive process that produced them is the thing mathematicians most want to understand. A process record of Ramanujan working, the pauses, the false starts, the sequences of symbolic manipulation, would be among the most valuable scientific documents in the history of mathematics.

**Information-theoretic point:** Ramanujan is the extreme case of process-artifact asymmetry. The artifact (a formula) is maximally compressed. The process that produced it is the entire scientific question. The loss is not sentimental. It is a loss of data that would advance mathematical understanding.

### Charles Darwin

Extensive notebooks, correspondence, and the manuscript drafts of the Origin. Scholars have studied his revision process through manuscript comparison. But the temporal dimension is absent: how long each revision took, whether he wrote fluently or haltingly, whether his writing process changed as the theory crystallized over twenty years. The notebooks show the sequence of ideas. They do not show the cognitive dynamics of how each idea emerged.

**Information-theoretic point:** Darwin's surviving manuscripts provide more process information than most historical figures, because multiple drafts survive. The scholarly value of comparing drafts is universally recognized. Process-level capture would provide the continuous record that draft comparison approximates discretely. The value of the discrete approximation (manuscript comparison) implies the value of the continuous record (keystroke-level process data).

## 4. The Information-Theoretic Framework

### 4.1 Lossy Compression Is One-Way

The artifact is a lossy compression of the process. Lossy compression discards information by design. The discarded information cannot be recovered from the compressed output. This is a mathematical property of lossy compression, not an engineering limitation.

In writing: the finished text is the compressed output. The discarded information includes the temporal ordering of production, the revision history, the pause structure, the motor dynamics, the retrieval patterns. A sentence that reads "The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog" is the same sentence whether it was produced in one fluent burst or assembled over five minutes of hesitation and revision. The text is identical. The process is different. The text is the lossy compression. The process information is discarded.

The one-way property means no future technology can recover the process from the text. You could train an LLM to guess plausible process records for historical texts, but the generated process would reflect the LLM's training distribution, not the actual cognitive process of the historical writer. The reconstruction would be a statistical hallucination, not a recovery.

### 4.2 Entropy Rates Differ by Record Type

**Artifacts**: Near-zero entropy rate once produced. A book does not change. It can be copied indefinitely with zero information loss. Storage technology improves monotonically. The survival probability of an artifact increases over time.

**Genomes**: Low entropy rate due to self-replication with error correction. DNA degrades but the information is redundantly encoded in every cell of the organism and in its descendants. Ancient DNA recovery is possible because the encoding is biological, not temporal.

**Process records**: Maximum entropy rate. The data exists only in the moment of production. It is temporally extended (a writing session unfolds over minutes to hours). It is not self-replicating. It is not stored by default. Without active capture at the moment of production, the information is lost at the speed of lived experience. The entropy rate of process data is bounded by the rate of human behavior, approximately one cognitive event per 100-500ms during active performance.

### 4.3 The Reconstruction Lower Bound

The Avatar provides an empirical lower bound on the information content of process records. If process-level measurements (motor timing, pause architecture, revision topology, vocabulary transitions) are sufficient to reconstruct writing behavior with fidelity F, then the measurements contain at least F bits of information about the behavior.

The reconstruction fidelity increases with corpus size (more data, better profile, denser Markov chain). The convergence trajectory is a measurement of how information-rich the process data is: fast convergence means the behavior is statistically regular and compressible; slow convergence means it is diverse and information-dense.

The residual (what the reconstruction cannot reproduce) is the information the measurements do not capture. This includes semantic coherence, argument structure, and the content-process coupling that reflects real-time cognitive engagement. The residual is the upper bound on what is lost even when the process is captured.

The complete information loss when process data is not captured at all is the sum of the reconstruction fidelity and the residual. For historical figures, both are lost.

## 5. Audience and Register

**Who this essay is for.** The broadest audience in the arc after Option E. Readers who think about information, preservation, and what is lost when data is not collected. Scientists, archivists, digital humanists, historians of science, philosophers of information. Technically literate general readers who recognize that data loss is irreversible and want a framework for thinking about what kinds of data matter.

**Register.** Essayistic. The information theory must be accessible to non-specialists. The historical examples must be vivid and specific. The connection to the present (AI mediation, closing window) must be direct rather than alarmist. The Avatar must be presented as an existence proof, not a product pitch.

Tonal models: James Gleick's *The Information* (history of information theory, accessible to general readers). Stewart Brand's Long Now Foundation essays (thinking about preservation on long timescales). Ted Chiang's "Exhalation" (a being studying its own cognitive process). Jorge Luis Borges's "The Library of Babel" (information, completeness, and loss as philosophical themes).

**Candidate venues.** *Aeon*, *Noema*, *The New Atlantis*, *Works in Progress*. Or a long-form essay in a science communication venue. Not a peer-reviewed journal. The contribution is the framework and the urgency argument, not empirical evidence.

**Length target.** 5,000 to 7,000 words.

## 6. Tentative Structure

### Opening: What We Lost When Franklin Died

Not what ideas he might have had. What cognitive data was erased. The temporal microstructure of how Benjamin Franklin thought, retrieved words, paused before sentences, revised arguments, shifted between domains. The data existed while he wrote. It was never captured. It is now permanently irretrievable. This is not a failure of archiving. It is a category of information that was never recorded.

The opening should make the reader feel the specific loss, not the generic "great minds are gone" sentiment. The specific loss is informational, not sentimental: process data that would answer scientific questions about cognitive architecture.

### Part 1: The Three Records

Recap from Option E, compressed: artifact, genome, process. The asymmetry. The lossy compression. The one-way property. Establish the information-theoretic framework without requiring prior reading.

### Part 2: The Historical Demonstrations

Franklin, Dickinson, Ramanujan, Darwin. Each illustrates a different dimension of process data and a different scientific question that the data would answer. Each is grounded in what scholars have actually tried to learn from incomplete process traces (Dickinson's variants, Darwin's drafts, Ramanujan's notebooks).

### Part 3: Why the Loss Is Irreversible

The information-theoretic argument. Lossy compression is one-way. Entropy rates differ by record type. No future technology can recover process data from artifacts. The reconstruction hallucination problem: an LLM generating "plausible" process records for historical texts is not recovery, it is fiction with statistical properties.

### Part 4: The Reconstruction Proof

The Avatar as existence proof. Process measurements reconstruct behavior. The fidelity is the lower bound on information content. The residual characterizes what is lost even when the process is captured. The complete loss for historical figures is total: both the reconstructible and the residual are gone.

Brief, concrete. Not a technical description of the system. A statement of what the reconstruction demonstrates: the data is demonstrably information-rich because you can rebuild the behavior from it.

### Part 5: The Closing Window

Connect to the present. AI mediation is making unmediated process data uncollectable. The people alive today who still write without AI assistance are producing process records that, if captured, would be among the most valuable cognitive data in history, because they represent the last generation of fully unmediated human writing behavior. The deflationary supply argument: the value of this data increases as it becomes scarcer.

### Part 6: What Preservation Requires

Not passive archiving. Active instrumentation. Purpose-built instruments that capture temporal microstructure during natural practice. The design constraints from Option D apply: unassisted input, process-level capture, n=1 baselines, practice autonomy. The instruments must exist before the window closes. The window is measured in years, not decades.

### Closing: The Record That Was Never Made

Return to Franklin. Imagine the record that would exist if Alice had been running while he wrote. Not the text (which survives). The temporal dynamics, the revision patterns, the motor fingerprint, the cognitive architecture expressed through the process of composition. That record would be, by any information-theoretic measure, more valuable than the text. The text is the compressed output. The record is the source. We have the compression. We will never have the source.

The essay should end not with a call to action but with the information-theoretic claim stated plainly: the loss of process data is irreversible, the data is demonstrably information-rich, the capture window is closing, and the instruments that would preserve it need to exist now.

## 7. Literatures to Engage

**7.1 Information theory and lossy compression.**
- Shannon (1948): foundational information theory
- Cover & Thomas: *Elements of Information Theory* (lossy source coding, rate-distortion)
- Kolmogorov complexity: the minimum description length of an object; process records have higher Kolmogorov complexity than artifacts because they encode more structure

**7.2 Cognitive process tracing in historical scholarship.**
- Genetic criticism (critique genetique): French literary theory tradition that studies manuscripts, drafts, and variants as evidence of the creative process. Foundational work by De Biasi, Gresillon, Ferrer. They are doing exactly what this essay argues for, but with the discrete approximation (surviving drafts) rather than the continuous record (process data).
- Dickinson manuscript studies: Martha Nell Smith, Marta Werner. The variant word choices as process traces.
- Darwin's notebooks: Howard Gruber, *Darwin on Man* (1974). Reconstructing the development of ideas from notebook sequences.
- Ramanujan studies: Bruce Berndt's systematic work on the notebooks. The process question that every Ramanujan scholar confronts.

**7.3 Digital preservation and data loss.**
- Library of Congress digital preservation program
- Long Now Foundation: Stewart Brand, Danny Hillis. Thinking about preservation on 10,000-year timescales.
- The "digital dark age" concept: Vint Cerf's warnings about format obsolescence. This essay extends the concept: the dark age is not just about format loss, it is about the non-collection of process data that was never recognized as valuable.

**7.4 Writing process research.**
- Inputlog (Leijten & Van Waes): keystroke logging as process capture. The technology that makes the essay's argument possible.
- Hayes & Flower (1980): cognitive process model of writing
- Galbraith (1999): knowledge-constituting model. Writing as thinking, not transcription.

**7.5 Cognitive reserve and the stakes of the argument.**
- Stern et al. (2023): cognitive reserve framework
- The retirement studies (Rohwedder & Willis): engagement builds reserve, disengagement erodes it
- Connection: process data doesn't just record cognition, it records the engagement that builds reserve

**7.6 The Avatar and reconstruction validity.**
- Option F (this project): reconstruction validity as validation method
- Analysis-by-synthesis (Stevens & Halle 1962): reconstruction as validation
- Condrey (2026): non-identifiability result, content-process binding

## 8. Pre-Drafting Work

1. **Read Shannon (1948) on lossy compression.** The rate-distortion function is the formal framework for the process-to-artifact compression argument. The essay should reference it without requiring the reader to understand it.

2. **Read genetic criticism.** De Biasi's *Genetique des textes* or a survey article. The critique genetique tradition is doing the humanities version of what this essay argues for, using surviving drafts as process traces. The essay should engage with this tradition as evidence that process data is already recognized as valuable in the humanities.

3. **Verify the Dickinson, Darwin, and Ramanujan examples.** Each must be grounded in what scholars have actually published about the process questions these figures raise. No invented claims about what the process data would show; only claims about what scholars have tried and failed to recover from artifact data.

4. **Formalize the deflationary supply argument.** The claim that unmediated process data appreciates as it becomes scarcer needs at minimum a qualitative economic framework. Is there precedent for "deflationary data" in the data economics literature?

5. **Decide on the role of the Avatar.** Options: (a) a single paragraph as existence proof, (b) a full section demonstrating reconstruction fidelity, (c) a footnote pointing to the reconstruction validity paper. Option (a) is probably right for an essayistic venue. The Avatar should demonstrate the claim that process data is information-rich. It should not become the subject.

6. **Decide on venue.** *Aeon* is the most likely venue for the register and length. *Noema* is an alternative. The essay's contribution is the information-theoretic framing of a preservation argument, which is conceptual, not empirical.

## 9. Relationship to the Arc

**Option E** (The Record): E names the process record and argues it is the most fundamental data. G quantifies the loss and argues it is irreversible. E is the "what." G is the "what happens without it."

**Option F** (Reconstruction Validity): F proves that process data contains enough structured information to reconstruct behavior. G uses this as the existence proof that the data is worth preserving. F provides the constructive argument; G provides the urgency argument.

**Option C** (Cognitive Reserve): C argues that AI offloading erodes cognitive reserve. G argues that the process data documenting whether reserve is being maintained or eroded is itself becoming uncollectable. C is about the cognitive stakes. G is about the informational stakes.

**Option B** (Construct Replacement): B argues that AI mediation replaces the cognitive construct underneath behavioral measurements. G argues that the data generated under unmediated conditions is irreplaceable once mediation becomes ubiquitous. B is about measurement validity. G is about data supply.

**The arc, in argument order:**
- B names the threat (construct replacement)
- A demonstrates it in one domain (keystrokes)
- C establishes the stakes (cognitive reserve)
- D derives the design constraints
- F proves the instrument works (reconstruction validity)
- E articulates the philosophical value (the process record)
- **G argues the loss is irreversible and the window is closing (information-theoretic urgency)**

G is the essay that makes the reader feel the cost of inaction. The other papers make intellectual arguments. G makes an informational argument: data that is not captured now is data that is permanently gone.

## 10. Open Questions for the Author

- **How much information theory to include?** The essay needs enough to ground the "irreversible loss" claim in mathematics, not just intuition. But too much formalism loses the general reader. The sweet spot is probably: state the lossy compression / one-way property clearly, reference Shannon and rate-distortion by name, and use the historical examples to make the abstract concrete. No equations in the main text.

- **Is the deflationary supply argument too economic for this essay?** The claim that unmediated process data appreciates in value as it becomes scarcer is an economic argument. It fits the urgency framing but may feel out of place in an essay about information theory and historical loss. Options: include it as a short section, relegate it to a footnote, or save it for a separate shorter piece.

- **How to handle the "every generation thinks its data is precious" objection.** The strongest counterargument: every era has believed its records were uniquely important. The Victorians feared that photography would replace painting. The mid-century feared that television would replace reading. Each fear was partly vindicated and partly wrong. The essay must explain why process data is categorically different from prior "we're losing something important" claims. The answer is information-theoretic: the loss of process data is mathematically irreversible in a way that the loss of, say, handwritten letters to email was not.

- **Whether to include living figures as well as historical ones.** The essay could note that people alive today are producing process data that, if captured, would be as valuable as Franklin's. This makes the argument personal rather than historical. Risk: it sounds like a product pitch for Alice. Mitigation: frame it as a general observation about any instrument that satisfies the design constraints, not about a specific system.

- **The ethics of cognitive data preservation.** Process records are intimate. They encode how a person thinks, not just what they think. Preserving them raises privacy, consent, and surveillance questions that the essay must at minimum name. The essay should not resolve these questions but should acknowledge that the preservation argument has ethical weight on both sides.

## 11. The Question Underneath

This essay is not ultimately about Franklin or Dickinson or Ramanujan. It is about a question that has not been asked at the right level of generality:

**What is the information-theoretic cost of not recording the process by which biological intelligences produce their outputs?**

The cost is irreversible. The data is demonstrably information-rich. The window for capturing it from unmediated human cognition is narrowing. The instruments that would capture it need to exist before the window closes.

The essay names this cost for the first time.
